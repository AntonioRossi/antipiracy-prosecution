## 1. Introduction

1.1 The applicant respectfully submits these informal comments for consideration by the designated Offices. No amendment is made herewith. The applicant intends to submit claims in the national and regional phases that state more expressly the technical relationship among the multi-camera production, edit-boundary variation, recipient association, and detection features discussed below.

1.2 The Written Opinion finds claims 1–18 novel and industrially applicable but considers them to lack inventive step starting from D1, US 2021/0352381 A1. The applicant respectfully submits that the aggregation analysis in the Written Opinion does not account for the functional relationship among the distinguishing features or for the different data flow used by D1.

## 2. The disclosed mechanism is based on camera-selection boundaries in multi-camera source material

2.1 D1 starts with a completed base copy and produces variants by replicating that copy and applying signal-domain transformations. The transformations include, among others, cropping or padding, brightness or color changes, rotation, scaling, time delay, frame-rate variation, banner-location changes, and watermark-like transformations. D1 also discloses embedding arbitrary identifiers in addition to a watermark ([0039]–[0041], [0078]). The applicant does not rely on an assertion that D1 is limited to one particular watermark format.

2.2 The present application uses a different production input and a different identifying quantity. A multi-camera production pipeline records director-commanded camera selections and their time codes in a structured list of instructions describing edits. A mate is generated by varying a recorded camera-selection boundary. In Example 2, the second camera remains selected for ten additional frames, the following camera selection starts ten frames later, and a later cut restores synchronization with the reference edit. The resulting mate therefore uses synchronized source-camera frames differently around a local edit boundary.

2.3 The disclosed architecture forms one co-operating mechanism:

- synchronized multi-camera source material supplies alternative frames around a camera-selection boundary;
- the production pipeline records the camera selections and boundary time codes in the structured edit list;
- mate generation varies selected boundary time codes to produce distinguishable camera-cut timing patterns;
- distribution associates delivered patterns, or manifests representing those patterns, with recipients; and
- detection observes camera-switch timings in suspected content and uses the same structural timing information to identify a delivered version and its associated recipient.

2.4 These stages are technically related because the quantity modified during production is the quantity recovered during detection. The mechanism can operate without requiring the edit-boundary variation itself to add data to the pixels, while remaining compatible with complementary watermarking or other processing. Its resistance extends to transformations that preserve enough of the temporal edit structure for the boundary pattern to be recovered; the application does not require that every conceivable targeted re-editing attack leave that structure intact.

2.5 The distinguishing features should accordingly be assessed as a combination producing a common technical effect rather than as an aggregation. This is consistent with Chapter 13 of the PCT International Search and Preliminary Examination Guidelines, which requires the claimed invention to be considered as a whole and without hindsight, and with the treatment of functionally interacting combinations in EPO Guidelines G-VII, 7.

## 3. D1 does not disclose or motivate the same data flow

3.1 D1's transformer receives the completed base copy. By contrast, locally moving a camera-selection boundary as disclosed in the present application uses synchronized material from an alternative camera that was not selected in the corresponding interval of the reference edit. D1 does not disclose supplying such alternate-camera source material to its transformer, recording director-commanded camera selections in an edit list, or rewriting a camera-selection boundary using the alternate source frames.

3.2 D1's transformations are used principally to impair alignment for collusion while preserving watermark detectability ([0016], [0026]–[0027], [0038]). D1 also discloses watermark-like arbitrary identifiers, but those remain signals embedded in the transformed copy. The present distinction is not that D1 lacks every type of identifier. It is that D1 does not disclose using the native multi-camera edit-boundary pattern as the version identifier and then recovering that pattern from suspected content through a record associating delivered patterns with recipients.

3.3 D1 permits transformations or parameters to be selected by client identity and/or randomly or pseudo-randomly ([0031], [0035], [0080]). Random selection does not, by itself, prevent a system from recording an assignment. The relevant absence is that D1 does not disclose the claimed closed loop in which the applied camera-boundary pattern is associated with a recipient and that same pattern is later measured in suspected content to resolve the recipient.

3.4 The scene-change discussion in D1, paragraph [0034], gives scene changes a different role. D1 aligns the fading or transition of a signal-domain transformation with a scene change so that the transformation transition is less noticeable. The scene boundary is a trigger or camouflage location for changing D1's transformation; it is not itself made into a recipient-specific camera-selection variable. The present application instead varies the timing of the camera-selection boundary and later detects that timing.

3.5 The other D1 passages mapped in the Written Opinion do not supply the missing mechanism. Paragraph [0037] concerns where D1's components may be placed in a distribution chain. Paragraph [0083] concerns applying image-domain transformations to segments. Neither passage discloses synchronized alternate-camera inputs, a structured list recording camera selections, local movement of a camera-selection boundary, or recovery of such a boundary pattern from suspected content.

3.6 D1's time-delay and frame-rate embodiments ([0048]–[0050], [0072], [0079]) do not change this conclusion. A uniform delay shifts an entire completed copy. A frame-rate transformation globally retimes frames of that copy. Neither operation, as disclosed in D1, selects alternate synchronized-camera frames to extend one camera shot in place of the beginning of another, while preserving or restoring a later boundary as in Example 2. Nor does D1 build its recipient association and forensic recovery around such locally varied camera-selection boundaries.

3.7 Starting from D1, the objective technical problem can therefore be expressed without assuming the solution: how to provide recipient-level differentiation and forensic recovery using production structure that remains observable after ordinary signal processing that preserves the edit sequence. D1 gives no reason to route synchronized multi-camera source feeds to its copy transformer, rewrite recorded camera-selection boundaries, and redesign detection around those boundaries. Doing so requires a different production input, representation, and recovery path, not merely selection of another item from D1's menu of completed-copy transformations.

## 4. Observations concerning Item VIII

4.1 Regarding how the time codes are altered and the resulting visual effect, the disclosed variation moves the actual camera-selection boundary rather than merely changing unused metadata. Example 2 provides a concrete local operation: one camera shot is extended by ten frames, the next begins correspondingly later, and a subsequent cut restores synchronization. The description characterizes the selected variations as subtle, minimal, or visually insignificant. Editorial acceptability depends on the selected source material and variation; the applicant does not submit that every arbitrary displacement is inherently imperceptible merely because the cameras recorded the same event.

4.2 Regarding “distinguishable” and “artificial intelligence algorithm,” the applicant intends to define distinguishability by the camera-cut timing pattern and to replace generic AI result language with supported processing operations. The application discloses detection of camera cuts or scene changes, derivation of time codes, comparison using perceptual hashes and sliding-window fuzzy matching, reconstruction of corresponding manifest information, and retrieval of the associated recipient from the ledger.

4.3 The applicant notes the observations concerning claim 7 and intends to amend or cancel unsupported result-oriented language in the national and regional phases.

## 5. Document D2

5.1 The applicant concurs with the categorization of D2, CN 117278762 A, as an “A” document. D2 concerns provenance along a codec chain using node metadata and watermarks. It does not disclose recipient-specific variation of multi-camera edit boundaries or recovery of a recipient from a delivered camera-cut timing pattern.

## 6. Conclusion

6.1 The application discloses a functionally integrated technical combination in which synchronized multi-camera source material enables local camera-selection-boundary variation; distribution associates the resulting structural timing patterns with recipients; and detection recovers the same timing information from suspected content. D1 and D2 do not disclose that combination, and the cited passages do not provide a reason to modify D1 toward it.

6.2 The applicant respectfully requests that the designated Offices consider this functional relationship when assessing inventive step. The applicant intends to present amended national and regional claims that expressly combine the supported production, local boundary-variation, association, and recovery features.
