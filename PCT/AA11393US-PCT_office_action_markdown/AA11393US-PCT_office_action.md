## Written Opinion — Separate Sheet

### Re Item V — Reasoned statement (novelty, inventive step, industrial applicability)

Reference is made to the following documents:

- **D1** — US 2021/352381 A1 (KILSTEIN GOLDA WEYL [IL] ET AL) 11 November 2021 (2021-11-11)
- **D2** — CN 117 278 762 A (ARBOO TECH CO LTD) 22 December 2023 (2023-12-22)

#### 1. Lack of novelty, inventive step

The references that are recited in the following section(s) are found in **D1**.

##### 1.1 Independent claim 1

> Anti-piracy system (100) identifying unauthorized distributions of streaming audio-video content; said anti-piracy system (100) comprising:

- ○ *"the transformer 120"* in **[0025]** and figure 1

> **(F1.1)** – a pipeline receiving live or pre-recorded video captured from one or more cameras (101)

— (**[0029]**: *"…a single media content storage 101…"* and the entire system function in the same paragraph and figure 1);

> **(F1.2)** said pipeline creating a reference audio-video content (1);
>
> **(F1.3)** switches between said cameras (101) being called camera cuts;
>
> – a mate creation component (110) generating at least one modified version of the video by automatically altering at least one camera-cut timing relative to the reference audio-video content (1), thereby producing one or more unique mates (111) of the reference audio-video content (1);

- ○ **[0037]**: *"It should be noted that though FIG. 2C illustrates a single server 230 C, components and/or functions of the server 230 C can be distributed to multiple devices. For example, while the variants generator 232 C is typically implemented in the video encoding headend, the sequence generator 234 may be implemented in other components of the media chain, e.g., at an encoder, a packager, a multiplexer, an over-the-top (OTT) origin server, an OTT playlist server, a content distribution network (CDN) server (central or edge), or a client device"*

> a mate (111) being a distinguishable version of the same underlying audio-video content (1);

- ○ **[0083]**: *"In one example, the set of segments can include the video stream, such as applying a fill mode transformation to modify stretch slightly so each client would have a slightly different image dimension to resist collusion attacks. In the example shown in FIG. 6, the transformation is applied segment-by-segment, so that each segment has a different transformation, and for each segment, the user receives one copy of this segment. In order to have a variety of transformations over time, the transformer changes transformations as time goes. As a result, the two copies of the video stream as shown in FIG. 6 may not be different in every point of time (e.g., segment 3 variant 630-B is in both copies), but along the timeline and over time, the two copies of the video stream are different to resist collusion attacks"*

> an ensemble (112) being a combination of both the reference audio-video content (1) and its mates (111);

- ○ **[0081]**: *"…generating multiple copies of the media content item by replicating and applying multiple transformations, wherein the multiple transformations applied to the multiple copies of the media content item are statistically different…."*

> – a distribution apparatus delivering one or more of said distinguishable versions to at least one recipient;
>
> – a record of associations, made by said distribution apparatus, between said distinguishable versions and respective recipients;

- ○ **[0031]**: *"…with reference to FIG. 6, in headend based transformation, the transformer 120 A determines which transformations to apply and generates multiple copies, e.g., two copies, for each segment and performs a different transformation on each of them. Each client device receives a sequence of transformed segments. For each segment, the client device choses one of the copies, e.g., based on the ID and/or randomly…"*

> **(F1.4)** – a detection apparatus applying an artificial intelligence algorithm to analyze a suspected unauthorized distribution by detecting camera-switch timings therein and to identify which of said distinguishable versions matches the suspected unauthorized distribution; said detection apparatus searching said record for a recipient associated to a distinguishable version matching the suspected unauthorized distribution.

- ○ **[0002]**: *"…in case a user leaks certain media content, his/her identity (ID) may be traced using embedded data. The watermark identifier is often encoded in an imperceptible manner while still allowing recovery of the watermark identifier even if the media content is modified."*

The subject-matter of the claim differs from D1 by the following **distinguishing features: F1.1, F1.2, F1.3, F1.4**.

The combination of said distinguishing features cannot be considered to be inventive for the following reasons:

- D1 is silent regarding multiple cameras — however, such system is well-known in the art;
- it is considered that using the multiple cameras do not have a technical synergy with the rest of the claims related to watermarking (modifying the content), besides "camera-switch timings" that corresponds to "scene change" in **[0034]**: *"…When the transformer 120 B applies a set of transformations, the transformations fade in and/or fade out or aligned with scene change for a smooth transition between the transformations…"*;
- since there is no technical synergy between this distinguishing features and the rest of the claim features, and using multiple cameras is well-known in the art, it is considered that these distinguishing features do not contribute to an inventive activity;
- moreover, merely referring to "applying an artificial intelligence" without further details cannot be considered to involve an inventive step.

Therefore the subject-matter of the claim is **not inventive (Article 33(3) PCT)**.

##### 1.2 Independent claim 18

Same reasons as for claim 1 apply *mutatis mutandis*, therefore the subject-matter of the claim is **not inventive (Article 33(3) PCT)**.

##### 1.3 Dependent claims

The subject-matter of the following claims is not inventive (Article 33(3) PCT).

- **1.3.1 — Claims 2-3, 7:** D1 is silent regarding using AI in this respect. As stated below in the clarity objections, the related features are broad, and it is considered that by merely referring the use of AI, without any specific details, does not involve an inventive activity.
- **1.3.2 — Claims 4, 7, 9-10:** D1 does not disclose specific pipelines and server clusters — however such hardware arrangements are well-known, and therefore they do not involve an inventive activity.
- **1.3.3 — Claim 5:** D1 relates to watermark — fingerprinting is a well-known alternative, statistical analysis is part of the most watermarking/fingerprinting techniques.
- **1.3.4 — Claim 6:** uni-casting (as multi-casting) is common general knowledge.
- **1.3.5 — Claim 8:** [0028], [0037].
- **1.3.6 — Claims 11-17:** "chunks" are implicit by reference to CDN in **[0028]**: *"…the system 100 may also include one or more encoders, packagers, and/or multiplexers, an OTT server, an OTT playlist server, and/or one or more CDNs…"*. Manifest files are common general knowledge.
- **1.3.7 — Claim 15:** **[0002]**: *"The process of hiding or embedding information in media content generally comes under the category of watermarking. Digital watermarking (sometimes also known as 'forensic watermarking') typically refers to the watermarking process where the embedded data identifies a media content item itself, as well as the content owner, operators, redistribution networks, and/or consumer devices associated with the distribution and consumption of the media content item. For example, in case a user leaks certain media content, his/her identity (ID) may be traced using embedded data. The watermark identifier is often encoded in an imperceptible manner while still allowing recovery of the watermark identifier even if the media content is modified."*
- **1.3.8 — Claims 16-17:** there is no disclosure of switching camera in D1, however it is considered not to involve an inventive activity (as argued for claim 1).
